About Makarska

Makarska is the center of the region known as Makarska Riviera. Makarska is located at the bottom of the Biokovo mountain, across the Brac island. It is the center both in administrative, economic and political sense. It is a center of culture, education, and since the 1950s, of tourism.

Today, Makarska has around 15000 inhabitants, which includes the picturesque places around Makarska, such as Veliko Brdo, Puharici, Kotisina and Makar.

Makarska is one of the most popular tourist destinations in Dalmatia. It is attractive because of its climate, great food, and many attractions.

History

The history of Makarska dates back to the 4th century. In a document by the Salona Council, a city of Mucrum is mentioned. In the itinerary of the Ravenat, in 7th century, it is mentioned as Aronia id est Mucru.

In the middle ages, there was a county of Mocro that was famous by its naval might, and it was a part of the Neretva dukedom. On September 17th, 887 AD, a Venetian doge by the name of Petar Kandijan was defeated in a large naval battle in front of Mokro.

The first time the current name, Makarska, is mentioned is around the year of 1502. When the Turks occupied, Makarska was a part of the Primorje province, and had three keeps to guard it. In 1684, Makarska became part of the Venetian republic, and during the Venetian hundred-year rule, the city's foundations were built.

When Venice fell, Austrians took the throne, imposed the Italianization policy, which made Italian the primary language. Makarska representatives in the Dalmatian assembly fought to introduce the Croatian language for regular use. In 1865, Makarska was one of the first to introduce the Croatian language.

During WWII, Makarska port served as HQ for Central Adriatic Naval Command. Later, it was moved to Split.

Town Square | Trg Fra Andrije Kacica Miosica

St. Mark's church is a baroque church situated at the top of the Kacic square in Makarska. In front of the church the statue of Andrija Kacic Miosic is located. It was created by Ivan Rendic, and it was put there in the year 1890. Behind the church there is an old school, which was built in the 19th century in neorenessance style. To the right of the church there is a water fountain, called Mletacka Cesma, which was built in 1775., when Makarska got drinking water which has inscriptions forbidding washing clothes and animal drinking.

Saint Mark's Church

St. Mark's church is a baroque church situated at the top of the Kacic square in Makarska. The build of the church started input started in the year 1700., but the church was never truly finished. On the right side to the entrance of the church, the bones of St. Clement are kept, who is the protector of Makarska. The bones were transported from Roman catacombs in 1725. The church was badly damaged in 1962., when an earthquake hit. When the repairs were made, the main altar was moved to a adjacent chapel. The altar is a beautiful masterpiece by Venetian artists. In the aftermath of the earthquake, the organs were damaged, and in 1970 were replaced.

Makarska Town Museum

The town museum of Makarska is a great place to go if you want to learn about the city history and culture. In the museum, you can find prehistorical artefacts, weapons from 16th century, and modern period artefacts. The Tonoli Palace, in which the museum is situated, is itself a historic treasure.

Malacological Museum

In the Franciscan monastery od Saint Mary, the Malacological Museum is situated. It was founded by a monk Friar Jure Radic and his associates. The museum was created with painstaking attention to scientific detail. It was founded to celebrate the malacology, the branch of zoology that studies molluscs. Friar Radic collected over 3000 shells and there is also a collection of invertebrae fossils. The collection has many specimens from around the world, as well some specimens from the Adriatic. There is also some flora of the Biokovo mountains and Adriatic region.

Museum of fish and crabs

Another museum in Makarska relating to the underwater life is the museum of fish and crabs. It celebrates the fish and crustaceans found exclusively in the Adriatic, mostly from the Makarska area. It houses more than 250 species of fish and crabs, and most of them are caught by local Makarska fishermen. There is a fish that has not been caught in Makarska area since 1980s, the sturgeon fish, which is two meters in length and weighs more than 20kg. Also, the museum houses various rare fish, large pen shells, sea turtles, bull sharks etc. The museum tells us the story of local fishermen and their adventures.

Vepric Shrine

On the entrance of Makarska, the Vepric shrine is located. It is situated in a natural setting, at the bottom of the hill. This holy place was founded in 1908 by Juraj Caric, a bishop who is buried in Vepric. The Lourdes sanctuary is similar to Vepric sanctuary because of the natural cave and the landscape. The shrine consists of the statue of Our Lady, a chapel, a open-air altar, and stations of the Way of the Cross.

Biokovo

Overlooking Makarska, the Biokovo mountain is situated. It is the second highest mountain range in Criatia. The highest point of Biokovo is Sveti Jure, which stands at 1762 meters high. Approximately 200 km square is a protected nature park Biokovo with around 1500 plants and animal species, and some are even endemic. Biokovo is a popular hiking destination, and there is Biokovo Nature Park center in downtown Makarska to aid hikers.

Tucepi

Tucepi is a small town south of Makarska, also a part of Makarska Riviera. It is a popular tourist destination due to the beautiful scenery, quiet enviroment and beautiful promenade.

Tucepi consist of Gornje Tucepi, Podpec, Covici, Srida Sela, Simici and Postup. All of the parts of Tucepi, except the main city, are situated at the foot of Biokovo.

Podgora

Podgora is also a part of Makarska Riviera, and it is a beautiful fishing port. On a hill above Podgora, there is a seagull wings monument, which was risen in 1962 to commemorate the anti-fascist Dalmatian navy in World War II. If you walk along the promenade and look into the houses' courtyards, you will see many Mediterranean plants, such as lemons, oranges, mandarins and many more.
